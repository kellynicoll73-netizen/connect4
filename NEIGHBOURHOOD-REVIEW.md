## Vancouver — West Side

### Kitsilano
**"Beach town meets bookshop city"** ✍

Rent: 1-bed ✓$2,350 · 2-bed †$3,200 · 3-bed †$4,100

Kitsilano runs along the water and knows exactly what it is. The seawall is the spine of daily life here — runners, cyclists, people walking dogs at 7am with coffee in hand. The beach is real and used, the farmers market is genuinely shopped at, and the coffee is taken seriously. There's money here, but it doesn't feel cold.

It rewards people who want the ocean and the city in the same breath and are willing to pay for the combination.

### Strathcona

Rent: 1-bed †$2,200 · 2-bed †$2,950 · 3-bed †$3,950

Victorian houses painted unexpected colours, community gardens tucked into vacant lots, and a social infrastructure built by people who chose to stay when staying wasn't fashionable. Strathcona is Vancouver's oldest neighbourhood and it carries that layered history visibly — the Japanese-Canadian block, the Chinese-Canadian block, the artists who arrived in the eighties and never left. ### Chinatown
**"A hundred years of history, a neighbourhood still negotiating its future"** ✍
Walk †9 · Transit †9 · Cycling †7 · Outdoors †5 · Diversity †10 · Safety †5 · Energy †7 · Fitness †5 · Quiet †5
Rent: 1-bed †$2,100 · 2-bed †$2,800 · 3-bed †$3,700

The Dr. Sun Yat-Sen Classical Chinese Garden sits behind a wall on Carrall Street, completely still. Half a block away, a new speakeasy opens in a space that was a dry goods shop for fifty years. Chinatown holds both of those things simultaneously — the century-old herbalist and the cocktail bar with no sign on the door — and the tension between them is what makes it genuinely interesting to live in right now.

People who can tolerate a neighbourhood in flux and find that interesting rather than unsettling. Proximity to Strathcona, Main Street and downtown without the cost of any of them.

Grocery options are genuinely limited — this is a practical consideration worth planning for, not just an atmosphere note. Safety perception is mixed. Gentrification is active and contested here and if that political dimension matters to you in either direction, it's worth knowing before you arrive.

> ✍ *"My family has had a business on Pender Street for forty years. I've watched three versions of this neighbourhood. I'm not sure what the fourth one will be."* — a second-generation herbalist on East Pender

## Burnaby

### Metrotown
**"The most efficient address in Metro Vancouver — and genuinely international"** ✍
Walk ✓10 · Transit ✓9 · Cycling †6 · Outdoors †5 · Diversity †9 · Safety †7 · Energy †8 · Fitness †8 · Quiet †3
Rent: 1-bed ✓$2,164 · 2-bed †$2,900 · 3-bed †$3,900

Metrotown scores a 97 walk score — higher than most of Vancouver proper — and the Expo Line SkyTrain is four minutes from almost anywhere in the neighbourhood. But the more interesting thing about Metrotown is what that walkability actually connects you to: Korean grocery stores with ingredients you won't find elsewhere in the region, Filipino bakeries that have been feeding the same families for thirty years, South Asian sweet shops and Japanese izakayas on the same block. This is one of the most genuinely international neighbourhoods in Metro Vancouver and the food reflects that without making a fuss about it.

People who want maximum city function at below-Vancouver prices and are comfortable in a dense, tower-heavy environment. The daily rhythm here is efficient — transit is fast, errands are walkable and the variety of food and retail within a ten-minute walk is genuinely hard to beat. If you find that kind of density energising rather than overwhelming, Metrotown delivers.

High-rise tower living is the dominant housing form and green space is limited. Noise and traffic are constants. This is a neighbourhood built around infrastructure and if that's not your idea of home, it'll feel like it from the first week.

> ✍ *"I have three Korean grocery stores, two Filipino bakeries and a SkyTrain stop within a five-minute walk. I do not miss paying Vancouver rent."* — an accountant who moved from Kitsilano two years ago

Surrey City Centre is a neighbourhood investing in itself. The King George SkyTrain station anchors an area that city planners envision as Metro Vancouver's second downtown — towers going up, a new library, an arts centre and a university campus already in place. Sapperton is a quiet, residential corner of New Westminster with a surprising amount of heritage housing stock — older character homes on generous lots, many with yards that would cost twice as much anywhere closer to Vancouver. The Royal Columbian Hospital anchors one end of the neighbourhood and a small but solid craft beer strip on Braid Street adds some evening life. The week here is calm and domestic in the best sense.

The curious thing about Sapperton is its transit paradox: a walk score of 26 (daily errands require a car) alongside a transit score of 75 (getting to downtown Vancouver by SkyTrain is genuinely easy). If you commute by transit and are happy to drive for groceries, this works well. If walkable errands matter as much as the commute, it's a harder fit.

Not much within walking distance beyond the immediate neighbourhood. The craft beer strip is worth knowing but narrow. Sapperton suits people who want a quiet residential base with a reliable commute more than an activated neighbourhood streetscape.

## Langley Township

### Fort Langley

Fort Langley looks like what Vancouver neighbourhoods aspire to be — brick storefronts from the 1800s, a Hudson's Bay fort that's now a national historic site and a Saturday market that people drive an hour to attend. But what makes it worth considering as a place to live rather than just visit is what it feels like the rest of the week: quiet streets, real yards, a community league that functions and a river trail that goes somewhere meaningful.

Life here runs on seasons and the village rhythm — the market fills up in summer, the trails are busy on autumn weekends and the community tends toward people who chose this deliberately and stayed. Residents have typically been here a long time. Housing is almost entirely detached homes and townhouses with proper outdoor space.

50-60 minutes to downtown Vancouver by car on a reasonable day and transit is not a practical commuting option. This neighbourhood makes sense for remote workers and people who have genuinely decided the city is behind them — not people testing the idea.

> ✍ *"I moved here from Yaletown five years ago. I have never once thought about going back."* — a copywriter who works remotely and runs the river trail on weekday mornings

## White Rock

### White Rock
**"Beachfront promenade, seaside town scale, a long way south"** ✍
Walk †6 · Transit †5 · Cycling †5 · Outdoors †8 · Diversity †5 · Safety †9 · Energy †4 · Fitness †4 · Quiet †8
Rent: 1-bed †$1,950 · 2-bed †$2,600 · 3-bed †$3,500

White Rock has a promenade, a pier and a beach that gets genuinely warm in August. The town above the hill operates at a pace that's deliberately slower than the rest of Metro Vancouver — unhurried, neighbourly and oriented around the water and the week's small rituals rather than the city's agenda. The demographic skews toward people who have made their peace with slowing down: retirees, remote workers and families who want outdoor space and a community that actually shows up for each other.

If the idea of walking the pier on a Tuesday morning before work — or instead of it — resonates, White Rock is worth serious consideration. Housing here is predominantly detached homes and townhouses with yards. The town feels genuinely like a place rather than a suburb.

An hour minimum to downtown Vancouver — plan on it and decide if the trade-off works before you sign anything. Summers bring day-trippers to the waterfront and the town gets busier than its year-round character suggests. Winters are quiet in a way that suits some people and doesn't suit others.

> ✍ *"I walk the pier every morning at 7am. In eight years I've missed maybe twenty days. That's why I'm here."* — a retired teacher who moved from Burnaby
